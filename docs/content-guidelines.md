# Edgion Content Guidelines

Edgion teaches news literacy through original background explainers, quizzes, and thought prompts. Content should help learners understand context without copying news articles or depending on a live news feed.

## Content Principles

- Write original explanations in Edgion's own words.
- Focus on background knowledge: history, institutions, stakeholders, concepts, and debate points.
- Keep the tone clear enough for high school and university learners.
- Avoid treating one viewpoint as the only reasonable answer.
- Help learners form an opinion instead of pushing a conclusion.

## What To Avoid

- Do not paste full news articles into modules.
- Do not copy long excerpts from articles, books, reports, or paid sources.
- Do not use unverified claims, rumors, or social media posts as facts.
- Do not present live breaking news as if it has already been fully understood.
- Do not add external APIs, RSS imports, or paid news services without a separate design review.

## Suggested Module Structure

Each module should include:

- A short topic summary.
- Four background sections:
  - History: how the issue developed.
  - System: what rules, institutions, or incentives shape it.
  - Stakeholders: who is affected and how.
  - Debate: what reasonable disagreements exist.
- A small timeline.
- A glossary of key terms.
- Two to three quiz items with explanations.
- Thought prompts that ask learners to build their own position.

Use [module-template.md](module-template.md) when drafting a new built-in module.

## Source Notes

For future modules, keep lightweight source notes during writing:

- Use official or primary sources when possible.
- Prefer government pages, public statistics, laws, institutional reports, and reputable educational materials.
- When using news articles, use them as topic signals, not as text to copy.
- Record source URLs in each module's `sourceNotes` field.
- Show learner-facing source notes concisely in Learn, without replacing the original explanation with a link list.

## Quiz Rules

- A quiz should check understanding, not trivia.
- Wrong choices should be plausible enough to make the learner think, but not misleading.
- Explanations should teach the concept again in plain language.
- Avoid questions that require remembering a single number unless the number is central to the topic.

## Thought Prompt Rules

- Prompts should invite a claim, reason, evidence, counterpoint, and next question.
- Prompts should make disagreement feel normal and useful.
- Avoid prompts that shame the learner for uncertainty.
- Encourage the learner to identify what they still need to check.

## Review Checklist

Before adding a new module, check:

- Is the explanation original?
- Is there enough background beyond the surface news event?
- Are multiple stakeholders represented?
- Does the debate section include a real tradeoff?
- Do quiz explanations help even when the learner guessed wrong?
- Are claims traceable to reliable source notes?
- Is the tone accessible without becoming careless?
